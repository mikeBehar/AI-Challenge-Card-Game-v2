Company Background
Company Profile
TrendSetters Retail
"Style That Fits Your Life"


Founded: 2010 (15 years old)
Headquarters: Portland, Oregon
Employees: 850 (including 45 retail locations + e-commerce team)
Revenue: $180M annually (down from $192M peak in 2022)
Stores: 45 physical locations across Western US
E-commerce: Growing but still only 35% of revenue
Customer Base: 2.3M active customers (defined as purchase in last 18 months)
Brand Position: "Accessible fashion for conscious consumers"—positioned between fast fashion (Shein, H&M) and premium brands
Business Model
TrendSetters built its reputation on:


Sustainable practices: Ethical sourcing, reduced waste
Personal service: Stylists in stores help customers find their look
Community focus: Local events, fashion workshops, style guides
Quality-to-price ratio: Better quality than fast fashion, more affordable than premium


The company successfully navigated the shift from pure retail to omnichannel, but growth has stalled as pure-play online competitors have gotten better at personalization and customer engagement.



The Business Problem
Current Performance Challenges
Marketing Effectiveness is Declining:


Email marketing: 2.8% click-through rate (industry average: 3.5%)
Social media engagement: Down 22% year-over-year
Customer acquisition cost (CAC): $68 per customer (up from $48 three years ago)
Marketing spend as % of revenue: 8.2% (above industry median of 6.5%)


Customer Retention is Weak:


68% of customers make only ONE purchase (never return)
Average customer lifetime value: $340 (down from $410 two years ago)
Repeat purchase rate: Only 32% make second purchase within 12 months
Cart abandonment rate: 73% (losing $4.2M annually in abandoned purchases)


Revenue Trends Are Concerning:


Three consecutive quarters of declining revenue
Same-store sales: Down 6% year-over-year
E-commerce growth rate: Slowing (was 25% YoY, now 12% YoY)
Market share in core demographic (women 25-45): Declining
Why This Is Happening
Market Research Findings (from Q3 2024 customer survey):


Overwhelming Generic Marketing:


64% of customers say TrendSetters' emails "aren't relevant to me"
71% have unsubscribed from at least one email list
Customers receive same promotions regardless of style preferences, size, or purchase history


Better Personalization Elsewhere:


82% say "other retailers seem to know what I like better than TrendSetters"
Competitors using AI show 3-5X higher engagement rates
Customers expect Netflix/Spotify-level personalization in retail too


Generational Divide:


Customers under 35: Want speed, convenience, AI recommendations, don't care much about privacy
Customers 45+: Value in-store experience, skeptical of data collection, privacy-conscious
Company trying to serve both groups with same approach


Lost in the Middle:


Fast fashion (Shein, ASOS) wins on price and trend speed
Premium brands (Everlane, Reformation) win on quality and brand story
TrendSetters no longer clearly differentiated
Board Mandate
At the August board meeting, directors gave management clear direction:


Improve customer lifetime value by 20% within 18 months
Return to revenue growth by Q2 2026
Find sustainable competitive advantage in personalization/customer experience


The board authorized up to $1.2M in technology investment to address these challenges.


CMO Sarah Chen took this as her mandate to explore AI personalization solutions.



The Proposed Solution: CustomerIQ™
What It Is
CustomerIQ™ is an AI-powered marketing personalization platform developed by Nexus Analytics, a Silicon Valley startup that's raised $85M in venture funding and serves 120+ mid-market retailers.
How It Works
Data Collection & Analysis:


Ingests customer data from multiple sources:


Purchase history (items, frequency, returns, sizes)
Browsing behavior (time on page, searches, abandoned carts)
Email engagement (opens, clicks, time of day)
Mobile app usage patterns
Social media activity (requires customer permission for Instagram/TikTok monitoring)
Location data (from mobile app—tracks store visits, shopping patterns)
In-store behavior (facial recognition cameras—tracks movement, dwell time, product engagement)
Third-party data (purchased from data brokers—demographic, psychographic, purchase behavior at other retailers)


Creates detailed customer profiles using machine learning:


Style preferences (bohemian, classic, edgy, minimalist, etc.)
Price sensitivity (discount hunter vs. full-price buyer)
Channel preferences (mobile vs. desktop, email vs. SMS)
Engagement patterns (morning browser, evening shopper, weekend buyer)
Life events (moving, new job, pregnancy—inferred from purchases and external data)


Personalization Output:


Email campaigns: Fully customized for each customer (subject line, products, timing, promotions)
Website experience: Dynamic homepage showing products AI predicts you'll like
Product recommendations: "You might also like..." based on style DNA, not just purchase history
Dynamic pricing: Adjusts prices based on individual willingness to pay (within 15% range)
Predictive engagement: Sends "we miss you" emails when AI predicts churn risk
Cross-channel coordination: Consistent personalized experience across email, web, mobile, in-store
Vendor's Promised Results
Based on Nexus Analytics' case studies with similar retailers:


40% increase in email click-through rates (from 2.8% to ~3.9%)
25% reduction in customer acquisition cost (from $68 to ~$51)
15% increase in average order value (from $85 to ~$98)
12% improvement in customer retention rate
ROI: "Pays for itself in 8 months" based on improved conversion and retention


Reference Customers:


StyleHub (similar size): Saw 38% email CTR improvement, 14% retention increase
UrbanThreads: Increased customer LTV from $280 to $385 in first year
FashionForward: Reduced cart abandonment from 71% to 54%

Key Decision Factors to Consider
Financial Analysis Questions
Total Cost of Ownership: Which option has the best 3-year TCO when you include opportunity costs?
Time to Payback: How long until each option generates enough revenue improvement to cover costs?
Risk-Adjusted ROI: If Option 2 has 40% chance of failure, how does that change expected value?
Downside Protection: Which option loses least money if it doesn't work out?
Competitive Positioning Questions
Time Pressure: How much does 6-month or 18-month delay cost in lost market share?
Sustainable Advantage: Which option creates hardest-to-copy competitive moat?
Category Definition: Are you competing on personalization sophistication or brand values?
Fast Follower vs. Leader: Is it better to be second with better implementation than first mover?
Organizational Capability Questions
Can We Recruit AI Talent?: Realistically, can TrendSetters hire and keep ML engineers in Portland?
Can We Execute Build?: Does organization have project management capability for 18-month build?
Can We Manage Vendor?: Do we have sophistication to negotiate with and manage Nexus Analytics?
Cultural Fit: Does AI-driven personalization align with TrendSetters' "conscious consumer" brand values?
Privacy & Ethics Questions
Facial Recognition: Is tracking customer movement in stores creepy or helpful? What do customers think?
Third-Party Data: Is buying data from brokers consistent with "conscious" brand values?
Dynamic Pricing: Is charging different prices to different customers fair or discriminatory?
Data Security: What happens if customer data is breached? How much reputational damage?
Informed Consent: Do customers really understand what data is collected and how it's used?
Regulatory Risk: What privacy regulations (CCPA, potential federal laws) might impact this?
Customer Perspective Questions
Generational Divide: How do you personalize for both 25-year-olds (want AI) and 55-year-olds (want privacy)?
Brand Trust: Could aggressive data collection undermine "conscious consumer" positioning?
Creep Factor: Is there a line where personalization becomes surveillance?
Customer Choice: Should customers be able to opt out of personalization? What % would opt out?
Vendor Relationship Questions (Critical After Case 1 Learning!)
Lock-In Risk: If we choose Option 1, how trapped are we? Can we leave in Year 3?
Pricing Power: What stops Nexus from tripling licensing fees after we're dependent?
Roadmap Control: What if Nexus prioritizes features we don't need or kills features we rely on?
Vendor Viability: What if Nexus runs out of VC money and goes out of business?
Data Hostage: If we leave, can we take our customer insights with us or start over?
Control Your Destiny: Brad's principle from Case 1—does this violate organizational autonomy?
